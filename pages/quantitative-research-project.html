Research Purpose & Methodology

This quantitative research project explores the implementation of smart city technologies in small towns. The research evaluates the transferability and scalability of IoT-powered smart bins from larger urban environments to smaller communities.

Key Methods: Survey research, statistical analysis, and technology acceptance modeling were employed to understand adoption patterns and community readiness.

Statistical Analysis & Findings

The research employed multiple statistical methods to validate findings and identify key predictors of technology adoption.

Statistical Methods Applied

  • Regression analysis revealed demographic predictors
  • Correlation tests showed relationships between variables
  • Age and education emerged as primary factors
  • Prior technology experience influenced adoption intent

Key Finding: The analysis revealed significant correlations between demographic factors and technology adoption rates. Age, education level, and prior technology experience emerged as primary predictors of adoption intent.

Theoretical Framework Application

Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)

The study applied TAM to understand how perceived usefulness and ease of use influence adoption decisions. Results supported the model's predictive validity.

Diffusion of Innovations Theory

The theory identified distinct adopter categories:

  • Innovators (8%): Early technology adopters
  • Early Adopters (18%): Community opinion leaders
  • Early Majority (34%): Pragmatic adopters
  • Late Majority (28%): Eventually adopt with social proof
  • Laggards (12%): Resistant to technological change
